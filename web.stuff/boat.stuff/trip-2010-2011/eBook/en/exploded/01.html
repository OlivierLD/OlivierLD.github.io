First Leg, San Francisco to Nuku-Hiva

From San Francisco to the Tropic of Cancer

Saturday, October 2nd, 2010
We left Oyster Point on October the 2nd, a Saturday, at 8:30 am, with the ebbing tide. The weather was what you can expect at that time of the year, gray and foggy. The wind picked up on the way, and we were sailing for real after the Bay Bridge. We came tacking close to the St Francis Yacht Club and Crissy Field, friends were gathered there, to wave us bye-bye... Janis and Michel took some pictures...
Outside the gate, that was a bit choppy, as we had the wind on the nose, and the current behind us. But that was all right. Cloudy sky, as expected. We had some porpoises around us.
We had all sails out, starboard tack.
We were heading, but the wind was nicely shifting clockwise, and at 5pm, we were able to begin to ease the sheets, I was not expecting this to happen that early.
At 6pm, a couple of whales (humpback whales) were jumping out of the water and hitting the surface with their tail and fins... Spectacular. One came close to the boat, blowing just next to Corine.
At night, there was no moon, and the wake was all shining with plankton. Soon after that, some porpoises came to play in the plankton as well.
To make sure she does not get sick, Corine had decided to sleep on the deck. She had her Afghan foulies on. Unexpectedly, the sea is not too rough, the night is quiet. It's good for a start. And the weather is not cold.

Sunday, October 3rd, 2010
That was a fast night, always around 6.5 or 7 knots. The wind dropped a bit in the morning, West swell. Dropped the mizzen, pretty much useless at this point of sail. Carrying Yankee, Staysail and full main, with a preventer.
The wind picked up again at sunset and shifted left. Big long swell from West-North-West.
At night, the sky was all clear except on the skyline, the Milky Way was like Las Vegas!

Monday, October 4th, 2010
The night was cool. Very nice weather this morning, quite pleasant!
The autopilot has been driving all the time. Done 140 miles during the last 24 hours, that's pretty good.

Tuesday, October 5th, 2010
Dropped the main at 1am, the wind shifted North and picked up, force 6. The wind from the North and the swell from the West, that generated quite a messy sea. The autopilot definitly feels better without the main in those conditions, and we're still doing a good 5 to 6 knots, that's good enough for cruising! The boat can for sure stand the main, but we would have to drive, as the autopilot does not anticipate the waves. The boat would be doing a lot of fish-tailing if the pilot had to drive.
The main came back up with two reefs at 7:30am, as the wind dropped.
The forecasts (the faxes and GRIBs we can receive) said the wind should shift North-East and drop a bit, tomorrow or the day after tomorrow.
Wind generator and solar panel did a good job today, there was no need to run the engine for the batteries.

Wednesday, October 6th, 2010
Loosed the two reefs in the main early this morning.
Corine was still sleeping on the deck, when a flying fish landed on her face. That was indeed an unexpected kiss, I believe.
As early as it was, it already smelt like Trade Wind!
Nice weather, but today the wind was light.

Thursday, October 7th, 2010
Very light night..., progressing slowly.
It was like if the wind wanted to come back from North-East..., and then we had South wind!
We were actually on the West side of a small high pressure - that's why we had this light weather - not mentioned on the faxes I had received. The barometer is going up too.
Perkins puff..., there was no bloody wind. The good thing though, is that the swell was gone with the wind.
But we did not break any distance record that day.

Friday, October 8th, 2010
The wind was all over the place, and it was raining.
Around 4am, the wind seemed to come back steady, from East, and then South-East, we had all sails out. Sailing is definitely much nicer with some wind! We were sailing in 8 to 10 knots of wind, at a close reach, that was nice, and the weather was then just great.
On the menu: shower (and warm!).
The night was clear. Yankee, staysail, and full main.

Saturday, October 9th, 2010
There was some kind of a nasty choppy sea... Like two trains of waves, North-East plus South-East.
One reef in the main at 8:45am. We've been at sea for now one week, we've done about 900 miles.
In the afternoon, the South-West swell seemed to calm down, and that was not too bad!
Corine was getting tanned on the deck.
That night she got her hand burnt with boiling water... Ooch.
She fixed it with the emergency kit, presented by Chantal & Dr Do.

From the Tropic of Cancer to the ITCZ

Sunday, October 10th, 2010
Yet another cotton night..., but no rain. The night was warmer that the previous ones.
We crossed the Tropic of Cancer around 5am. The weather is horrible, gray, cloudy. How tropical!
Temperature left aside, it was just like Brittany (in November).

Monday, October 11th, 2010
Still a cotton night, no horizon, little visibility, no stars... And weird seas.
Loosed the reef in the main in the morning, weather was gray, but it was warm. It cleared up from time to time, that was nice.
Corine was still sleeping in the cockpit, with her yoga mattress, extra blanket..., she had her own little setup.

Tuesday, October 12th, 2010
The wind was now blowing from the East. The mizzen was back up, and back down in the afternoon, as the wind shifted North-East.
Corine made some bread, and some cake.
Wind was blowing 4 to 5 Beaufort. En route with Yankee, Staysail, and one reef in the main', at more than 6 knots. It was a nice Trade Wind day, weather was warm, water temperature was now 25°C (77°F), compare to what is was in the San Francisco Bay, 12.5°C (54.5°F).
The moon was here that night, this was a nice one.
Took the second reef in the main for the night, to relieve the autopilot.

Wednesday, October 13th, 2010
Loosed one reef at 5am. Overcast sky.
Put the reef back in the main, the autopilot felt much better.
We had squalls all along the day, mostly rain, but up to 35 knots. That kind of weather keeps you on your toes, we're maneuvring non-stop.

Thursday, October 14th, 2010
Doldrums! In a squall, the wind suddenly shifted South-East.
After the fax, we were just having a small tropical low, not the ITCZ (Inter Tropical Convergence Zone) yet...
At 2pm, we had all sails out. And at 4pm, we dropped the mizzen. Blowing from the East..., at 7pm, we had two reefs in the main.
It was happening just like in the books, the wind was undecided, implying a lot of maneuvers. I was not getting rusted.
Still some squalls. Dropped the main in a strong one, and put it back up after it.

Friday, October 15th, 2010
A rodeo night! We were bouncing from squall to squall between 8pm yesterday and 4am today, the squalls were back to back. We had a great rainbow for sunrise though.
The wind was supposed to drop a bit during the day, but it stayed like that all day long. We were still having puffs above 30 knots.
The sky cleared up after sunset, and the weather felt better.
Still no squall at midnight...

Saturday, October 16th, 2010
First squall at 2am, from the South-East, not too strong.
We were in the ITCZ at 12:45pm, definitely! It's like a huge curtain of clouds and rain. The wind increased and decreased, shifted left and right..., all by the book! Puffs above 30 knots.
A lot of birds around us.
At 8pm, it seemed we were done with the ITCZ, that was faster than expected...
En route, Yankee, Staysail, two reefs in the main, one reef in the mizzen, good balance.

From the ITCZ to the Equator

Sunday, October 17th, 2010
The wind was all over the place again. The waves as well, what a night!
Still many birds around us, mostly petrels and shearwaters.
At 3pm, the wind was shifting the right way, and we were right on the route.
But as soon as the sun was down, the big mess was back again.

Monday, October 18th, 2010
Squalls again and again. This morning, we had a real Viking weather, we would not have been surprised to get some snow.
It cleared up around noon.
We caught a nice mahi-mahi, but this bastard got off the hook just before making it in the cockpit, ah shit!
We put the line back in the water after that, catching nothing.
But when I pulled the line back in at night, I saw that all the bottom (hook & lure) was gone, clearly cut. We must have caught something with teeth...
Big mess again after sunset.

Tuesday, October 19th, 2010
The wind came back from the South-East, then South-South-East, then East-South-East.
And then we were heading, against a force 6 Beaufort.
Dolphins at 3pm.
The boat was behaving nicely, but it was shaking a lot.
Clear sky that night.

Wednesday, October 20th, 2010
It seemed we had some current, taking us West...
After doing the math, we did have current, about 3 knots, which is quite big. Same result on the other tack, that seemed to be real. Amazing. I checked, double checked and triple checked, with all the navigation means I could think of, and always found the same result (which is reassuring, even if it is surprising).
At 11pm, no wind... I checked: the Pilot Charts say 0% of calm... Guaranteed (or your money back).

Thursday, October 21st, 2010
The current was reaching 4.5 knots (in the 313), which is really huge! We were actually supposed to have the equatorial counter-current, taking us East. We made no headway during the last day.
All those weird things were confirmed by the sextant, and traditional techniques...
The current seemed to weaken a bit in the morning, good! The wind slowly shifted South-East (still a lot of South in it).
At 4pm, there was a current, 1 knot, but taking us South!
Wind force 6, from the South-East.

Friday, October 22nd, 2010
Shaky before morning..., better after that (sea and wind almost in the same direction). Yankee, Staysail and mizzen.
Set the main with two reefs at 8pm.

Saturday, October 23rd, 2010
Nice night...
We crossed the line at 6:52am (PDT), by 138°11' W. At noon, we opened the bottle of wine presented by Michel for the circumstance, Saint Estephe 2003. It's good to be South!
Nice weather, nice sea, good progression, all fine!
More than 5 knots during the last 24 hours.

From the Equator to Nuku-Hiva

Sunday, October 24th, 2010
Calm night.
Wind blowing from East to East-South-East during the day.
Gusty night after that.

Monday, October 25th, 2010
There was some current with us.
120 miles on the water, about 150 on the ground, during the last 24 hours.
Less than 200 miles to go!

Tuesday, October 26th, 2010
Still some current with us.
Seven flying fish on the deck this morning!
All sails out, including the mizzen staysail, starboard tack, at 2pm. At 3pm, we were close to the Clark Bank. The wind was blowing from the North-East (I was expecting more like South-East).
6:40pm: Land Ho! Corine saw it first. I did not believe it, but that was true, there was more than 30 miles of visibility. We were seeing Nuku-Hiva and Ua-Huka.
Dolphins joined us for sunset. Very nice night after that.
Sailing along the East coast Nuku-Hiva, that is quite wild. It was amazing to see that there was absolutely no light at all on the island. Absolutely none.

Wednesday, October 27th, 2010
With the wind we had last night, we were early; we rounded the Cape Martin, and after that we tacked a couple of times in front of Taiohae, waiting for the sun to show up.
We entered the bay just after sunrise, and we dropped the anchor in about 40 feet of water.
The Bay is the crater of an ancient volcano, collapsed into the sea.
We are entering the crater of a volcano, by boat...
The landscape is beyond fantastic.

Summary of the first leg
  • First, we've sailed more with the boat during the past 25 days than in the past 6 years...
  • We've lost a winch handle - it still does not float very long.
  • The leg can clearly be splitted in four different sections:
    1. San Francisco to the Tropic of Cancer - Starting from fall in the Bay
    2. Tropic of Cancer to the ITCZ - Changing wind, unstable conditions
    3. ITCZ to the Equator - Improving conditions, but surprising currents
    4. Equator to Nuku-Hiva - The best part, all downhill!
  • Behavior of the boat under sails
    • It is indeed a very pleasant boat, even in rough seas, it never bangs in the waves, and it is very unusual to have water in the cockpit.
    • There is definitely no need to carry too much canvas (specially upwind, of course), it does not help
    • When heading, keep the mizzen up as long as possible, reduce or drop the main instead of the mizzen; it really helps to keep the bow up.
    • We carried all the time in front the yankee and staysail.
  • Forecasts, etc.
    • In tropical zone, on the logbook, the columns "visibility", "barometer", and "clouds" are useless.
    • The GRIB files are not that reliable in this same zone, too generic.
  • We had to deal with conditions we wouldn't have put the boat in, if we had had a choice. What we get out of that is an increased confidence in its possibilities and capacities. The boat can take it, and that's good!
  • We (almost) never needed the genoa during this leg, it stayed in its bag.
